Yuzu — Corporate & Commercialisation Strategy

Local-only strategy notes (gitignored via *.local.md). Captures the commercialisation thinking as of 2026-04-21 so it doesn't live only in chat history. Update in place as decisions firm up.

The model

Open-source code + paid support, not dual-license. Same bits ship to everyone; paid customers buy people, SLA, and sponsored development that lands back in OSS. RedHat / Canonical / HashiCorp-pre-BSL lineage, not the Sentry / MongoDB / Elastic dual-license / source-available lineage.

What this model is good at

  • Regulated / sovereign buyers (gov, defence, healthcare, finance, EU) get full source audit rights and a throat to choke. That combination is uniquely suited to Yuzu's endpoint-management positioning — these buyers dislike black boxes running on every endpoint they own.
  • Engineering story is simpler. One codebase. No entitlement server. No licence-key infra. No enterprise test matrix. No feature-gate litigation over what goes where.
  • Sponsored development is a legit additional revenue lane. Collabora, Igalia, EDB survive on exactly that pattern. Governments in particular buy sponsored development more easily than pure support contracts because the line-item is legible to their procurement processes.

What this model is bad at

  • Margins are thin. You sell labour, not bits. RedHat was the most successful pure-support company ever and capped at ~$3B ARR before IBM; most plays top out at $5–50M.
  • No natural "upgrade to paid" moment mid-usage. Every deal is a full top-of-funnel cycle, typically 3–9 months.
  • Free-riders are the default. Conversion from users → paid is typically <1%.
  • The moat is a named team. A well-capitalised fork ("Yuzu Pro", same bits, someone else's support contract) erodes differentiation instantly unless you are the canonical maintainer with public visibility.

Consultancy as delivery partner

Nathan holds 50% (largest share of four partners) in a 28-person consulting company. Currently profitable at ~£2k/day with two people on the bench. This materially changes the risk picture.

What it unblocks

  • The "named team / single throat" objection collapses. Procurement sees a 28-person delivery org, not a solo maintainer. Put the consultancy on the customer assurance page as the support-delivery entity.
  • SOC 2 funding (~£30–50k all-in for Type I) becomes non-blocking. Fund from consulting margin as operational infrastructure, not from speculative Yuzu revenue.
  • Bench rotation covers the immediate ramp work (support ops buildout, SOC 2 evidence collection, customer assurance docs, licensing / pricing copy) without new hires. ~2 bench consultants for 6–8 weeks gets through most of it.
  • Contracts, invoicing, HR, legal, sales machinery already exist. Not bootstrapping operations — grafting a product onto an existing delivery org.

What it does not unblock

  • Licence choice is still a Nathan-hours-with-a-lawyer decision.
  • Public positioning / Nathan-as-named-expert cannot be consultancy-delegated. It's your face on the market.
  • Product management is a different muscle than consulting delivery. Bench rotation fills support-engineer and docs slots; it doesn't fill the "who owns the roadmap" slot. That stays Nathan.

Corporate structure decision

The single highest-leverage decision to make before rotating consultancy bench onto Yuzu work. Three patterns:

  1. Yuzu lives inside the consultancy. Simplest short-term. - Problem: conflates unit economics that shouldn't be conflated (subscription vs T&M consulting). - Problem: friction if the other 3 partners don't want to subsidise product R&D from consulting margin. - Problem: complicates any future Yuzu-specific capital raise or exit.
  2. Yuzu as separate entity (NewCo); consultancy is first support-delivery partner. Most common pattern for this situation. - NewCo owns IP, trademark, and subscription revenue. - Consultancy is a named delivery partner on a Master Services Agreement with margin sharing. - Cleaner governance, cleaner capital raises, cleaner exit. - Cost: ~£3–10k UK corporate legal + a few weeks of setup.
  3. Yuzu owned by Nathan personally. Worst pattern unless the other partners specifically want it. - UK tax-hostile (personal income instead of corporation tax). - Muddy on IP when consultancy engineers contribute under their existing employment contracts. - Weak investor optics.

Recommendation: Pattern 2. Make the call before rotating consultancy bench onto Yuzu. IP contributions made under ambiguous ownership get cleaned up later via assignment deeds, but the cleanup conversation gets friction-y once commercial momentum exists. Do it while it's still theoretical.

Partner alignment

Parallel conversation with the other 3 consultancy partners:

"I want the consultancy to become Yuzu's first support-delivery partner. Here's the structure, the margin arrangement, how bench allocation changes over 6–12 months, and what happens if Yuzu revenue grows past consulting revenue."

50% + largest-share lets Nathan push through, but goodwill in a 28-person partnership matters a lot. The partners may not have signed up to be a product company. Get them bought in explicitly, ideally before external legal structures are set up.

Sponsored development as a product

Don't treat this as "you can pay us to build stuff." Publish an actual intake process:

  • How to propose a feature.
  • How it gets priced (T&M vs fixed-scope).
  • SLA on proposal turnaround.
  • Commitment that the feature lands in OSS under the project licence.
  • Which categories will be refused (proprietary lock-in features, things that fork governance).

Governments and regulated buyers read this more easily than support contracts because the line-item is legible to their procurement processes.

Licence defence against cloud strip-mining

Currently Apache 2.0, which means a well-capitalised cloud provider can take the code, repackage it as a managed service, and compete without contributing back. Historical examples: AWS → Elasticsearch, AWS → MongoDB, AWS → Redis.

Realistic lesson from all three: a determined cloud provider will build a competing service regardless of licence. The licence choice only determines whether they have to re-implement (costly) or can fork directly (free). The trademark determines what they can call it. The community governance determines who the "canonical" project is.

Options, ranked for a support-revenue model

  1. Apache 2.0 + CLA + registered trademark (recommended starting state) - Keep the maximally permissive licence for now; adoption matters more than defence at current scale. - Add a Contributor Licence Agreement (CLA) so relicensing stays an option later. Without a CLA, every contributor has copyright on their lines and you cannot unilaterally move to BSL / ELv2 / AGPL later. With a CLA, you preserve the option. Do this before external contributors exist — cost now is zero, cost later is chasing signatures from people who have left the industry. - Register "Yuzu" as a trademark (UK ~£200, EU ~€850, US ~$350). Publish a trademark policy. Blocks "AWS Yuzu Service" naming even if someone forks the code. - Watch the market. Revisit if Yuzu crosses ~100 paying customers or a cloud provider starts shadowing the roadmap.

  2. AGPL-3.0 - Strongest still-OSI-approved defence. Forces any SaaS provider to open-source their entire management plane if they run modified Yuzu. - Cost: meaningful percentage of enterprises have blanket policies against AGPL internal use. Cuts adoption by ~20–40% depending on vertical. - Legal ambiguity on "linking" vs "using" has never been fully tested in court. Procurement teams know this and sometimes treat it like SSPL risk anyway. - Fit: if sovereign/gov buyers dominate your pipeline and fortune 500 adoption doesn't matter, AGPL is the cleanest still-OSI answer.

  3. BSL 1.1 with 4-year conversion to Apache 2 - HashiCorp's model. Fully prohibits commercial competing services; each version converts to Apache 2 on its 4-year anniversary. - Not OSI "open source" — the marketing claim "open source" becomes contested. Procurement usually accepts "source available" once they understand the terms, but the negotiation is longer. - Fit: if you want an explicit anti-cloud clause but still want eventual OSS status for old versions.

  4. Elastic Licence v2 (ELv2) - Simplest anti-cloud clause: three restrictions (no managed service, no circumventing licence keys, no obscuring licensing). - Not OSI. Used by Elastic, Sentry (before FSL), Redis. - Fit: if you want the cloud protection without BSL's time-delay complexity.

  5. Functional Source Licence (FSL) - 2023-ish, Sentry's replacement for ELv2. 2 years of restricted use → converts to Apache 2 or MIT. - Simpler than BSL; shorter conversion window. - Less battle-tested in procurement conversations than BSL or ELv2.

Recommendation

Do option 1 now, re-evaluate in 12–18 months:

  • CLA is the single highest-leverage move. Preserves the relicense option. Without it, every future licence conversation gets harder with every new contributor. Cheapest possible insurance against the cloud-repackaging risk, regardless of which direction you eventually take.
  • Trademark registration is cheap and fast. Blocks the brand-fork vector even if the code-fork vector remains open.
  • Don't pre-emptively move to AGPL / BSL / ELv2. The cost is real (adoption hit, procurement friction, signalling "this is a defensively-licensed project"), and the defence is premature. AWS doesn't repackage code that isn't already profitable. Elasticsearch was $100M+ ARR when AWS forked it. Yuzu isn't at repackaging risk until it's commercially successful — and by then, the CLA gives you the option to relicense from a position of strength.

Concrete triggers for relicensing

Revisit the licence decision (probably to ELv2 or BSL) when any of:

  • A cloud provider starts shadowing the roadmap (PR, blog posts, launch announcements).
  • Yuzu crosses ~100 paying customers and the roadmap is publicly visible enough to be a target.
  • A specific customer asks for an "anti-cloud" clause in their contract (this happens at the sovereign / gov tier).
  • You raise Yuzu-specific capital and investors push for source-available (common).

Priority order

  1. Week 1: corporate structure decision + partner alignment conversation (parallel).
  2. Week 1: CLA + trademark registration kick-off.
  3. Week 1–2: licence verification (done — Apache 2.0 confirmed) and trademark application filed.
  4. Week 2–4: engage SOC 2 auditor (Vanta or Drata + Prescient / Johanson / A-LIGN); assign 1 bench consultant to support-ops buildout (ticket system, KB, SLA tracking, runbooks); assign 1 bench consultant to SOC 2 evidence collection.
  5. Week 4–6: draft pricing page + customer assurance one-pager.
  6. Week 6+: start the pilot conversations.

Open questions

  • Which UK corporate counsel? Cost envelope ~£5–10k for NewCo incorporation + IP assignment from consultancy/Nathan + MSA between NewCo and consultancy + CLA template.
  • Which CLA template? Apache Foundation iCLA is the gold standard; alternatives are Harmony (permissive) and Google's CLA. DCO (Developer Certificate of Origin) is a lighter-weight alternative that does not preserve relicense rights — fine for adoption optics, useless for the cloud-defence case. Pick iCLA or Harmony.
  • Which SOC 2 tooling — Vanta vs Drata? Vanta is more mature for SaaS controls; Drata is slightly cheaper and has better integration with smaller auditor panels. Both will work.
  • Trademark scope — UK only initially or file through Madrid Protocol for international? Answer depends on expected first-customer geography.
